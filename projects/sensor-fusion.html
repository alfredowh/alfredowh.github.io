Sensor Fusion of Radar and Camera for Road User Detection

CARISSMA Automotive Research Institute · Student Research Assistant · 03/2024 – 03/2025

  • Sensor Fusion
  • FMCW Radar
  • Edge Deployment (Jetson)
  • Python
  • PyTorch

Overview

  • Goal: fuse a 77 GHz FMCW radar with an RGB camera so road-user detection (car, pedestrian, cyclist, truck) stays robust in fog, rain, and low light — exactly where camera-only detectors degrade sharply.
  • Model: Tiny YOLOv7, chosen so the fused model still fits on an NVIDIA Jetson for onboard, real-time inference.
  • Challenge: radar is sparse and texture-less, so the gain from fusing it in has to survive real deployment constraints — edge-sized model, real weather conditions.

Why Fuse Radar and Camera?

  • Radar: directly measures distance and radial velocity for every return, and its longer wavelength shrugs off fog, rain, and darkness — but returns are sparse and carry no texture or shape.
  • Camera: dense, semantically rich, cheap — but purely passive and visibility-limited, so accuracy collapses exactly when radar stays reliable.
  • Idea: let the camera supply appearance and the radar supply distance/velocity + weather robustness, so the fused model degrades more gracefully than either sensor alone.

Early Fusion: Projecting Radar onto the Image

  • Dataset: View of Delft, whose radar unit is the same 77 GHz FMCW automotive radar family as the test rig above.
  • Projection: each 3D radar point is projected onto the image plane via the calibrated extrinsics.
  • Early fusion: radar channels (e.g. distance, velocity) sit stacked alongside the RGB channels as direct input to Tiny YOLOv7.

Experiments

  • Channel combinations — RGB vs. RGB stacked with different radar channels (distance, radial velocity, absolute/ego-motion-compensated velocity), down to a compact 2-channel input of 1 grayscale/RGB channel + 1 radar-distance channel.
  • Point-cloud dilation — a projected radar point covers only a few pixels; dilating and scaling up each point's footprint before stacking makes the sparse radar channel easier for the CNN to pick up.
  • Elevation augmentation — jittering each radar point's projected position vertically to counter radar's coarse elevation resolution and calibration/projection noise, densifying the radar channel around each true detection.
  • Multi-sweep accumulation — stacking multiple radar sweeps (vs. a single sweep) to densify the point cloud before projection.

Evaluation: Robustness in Fog

All variants benchmarked against RGB-only Tiny YOLOv7 across four simulated conditions — clear, light fog, medium fog, strong fog — per class (car, pedestrian, cyclist, truck).

  • Clear: RGB-only is competitive; fusion variants add little.
  • As fog thickens: most fusion variants (plain fusion, dilation, absolute-velocity) fall to roughly RGB-level or below — extra radar channels alone don't guarantee a gain.
  • Fusion + elevation augmentation is the consistent exception, pulling ahead across classes as conditions worsen, most visibly on cyclist and truck.
