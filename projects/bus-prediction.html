Overview

  • Project: Digitaler Zwilling des Ingolstädter ÖPNV — a digital twin of Ingolstadt's public transport, forecasting passenger information from a SUMO-simulated bus network.
  • Sub-teams split by pipeline stage: data generation from SUMO, forecasting models, and passenger information visualization.
  • My team was part of the forecasting sub-team, owning ConvLSTM — capturing how passenger demand propagates spatially across connected stops and bus lines, not just over time — alongside teammates building LSTM, a Deep Belief Network (DBN), and Random Forest baselines.

ConvLSTM: Architecture

ConvLSTM replaces the fully-connected gates of a standard LSTM with convolutional ones, so spatial correlation between neighboring stops and routes is modeled alongside the temporal dynamics. The encoder–decoder design follows Petersen et al.'s: the encoder folds a sequence of link-level inputs into a context vector, which the decoder unrolls into the multi-step forecast.

Multi-step rollout strategy

StrategyIdea
Auto-regressive Own prediction fed back in as the next input — matches inference, but errors can compound.
Teacher forcing Ground truth fed in at every training step — stable, but mismatches inference (exposure bias).
Scheduled sampling Anneals from ground truth to own predictions during training, bridging the two.

Model depth

Single-layer vs. dual-layer ConvLSTM stacks were compared, trading added capacity against overfitting risk on a comparatively small, simulation-generated dataset.

Training Setup

  • Time-series-aware split — train/val/test split chronologically, not shuffled, so evaluation always comes after training in time.
  • Automated hyperparameter tuning with Ray Tune (layer depth, hidden channels, learning rate, sampling schedule), every run tracked and compared in MLflow.
  • Baselines — historical average, feed-forward neural network (FFNN), and a plain LSTM as reference points for ConvLSTM.

Results: ConvLSTM vs. Baselines

Models are evaluated on SUMO-simulated datasets and the real-world Qbuzz dataset.

Cross-Model Evaluation

The real test was against the other sub-teams' models — DBN and Random Forest (RFNN) — on three bus lines with different service frequencies and route characteristics (Line 10, Line 70, Line S2).
