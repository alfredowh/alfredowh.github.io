Model Compression: Pruning & Knowledge Distillation

M.Sc. Artificial Intelligence, TH Ingolstadt · Course Project

  • Pruning
  • Knowledge Distillation
  • MobileNetV2
  • PyTorch

Overview

MobileNetV2 is already a lightweight image classifier (3.5M parameters, vs. 61M for AlexNet and 138M for VGG), pretrained on ImageNet1k's 1,000 classes with 71.87% Top-1 / 90.31% Top-5 accuracy. The goal here was to push it further: find the best trade-off between model size, inference speed, and accuracy through channel-level pruning, and recover the resulting accuracy loss through fine-tuning strategies including retraining, iterative pruning, and knowledge distillation. The central challenge is that MobileNetV2 is already compact by design, leaving little redundancy to remove before accuracy degrades sharply.

Pruning Methodology

Structured pruning was chosen over fine-grained/unstructured pruning because it speeds up inference on standard hardware without needing sparse-matrix support. Specifically, channel-level pruning was applied to MobileNetV2's convolution layers — removing entire channels rather than individual weights.

Criterion — which channels to remove

CriterionIdea
Magnitude-based Rank channels by their L1/L2 weight norm — larger absolute weights are assumed more important.
Scaling-based Rank channels by their batch-norm scale (γ). Since y = γx̂ + β, a γ close to zero means that channel barely contributes to the output.

Type — how the pruning ratio is applied

TypeIdea
Layer-wise (uniform) Every layer is pruned by the same fixed ratio.
Global A single threshold is set from the ratio applied to all layers' sorted criterion values pooled together, so layers are pruned by different amounts depending on where their channels rank overall.

Combining both axes gives four pruning variants (uniform/global × magnitude/scaling), each tested at ratios [0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9].

Pruning alone collapses accuracy

Applied directly with no retraining, all four variants destroyed accuracy almost immediately — Top-1 dropped below 2% even at a 10% pruning ratio. A per-layer sensitivity analysis confirmed why: MobileNetV2's channels are tightly coupled, so most conv layers lose the majority of their accuracy contribution within the first 10–30% pruned. This made fine-tuning after pruning essential.

Fine-Tuning

Three fine-tuning strategies were explored to recover accuracy after pruning: plain retraining at a fixed ratio, iterative pruning (alternating pruning and retraining in stages to reach a target ratio gradually), and knowledge distillation (using the unpruned model as a teacher during retraining).

Setup — keeping the comparison fair

Since the goal was to compare pruning/fine-tuning strategies against each other rather than to chase the highest possible accuracy, every experiment shared a fixed training budget and configuration so that differences in results reflect the method, not incidental training randomness:

  • Same fixed data split — ImageNet1k's validation set (50,000 images, 50/class) was split 80/20 into train/test once and reused across all experiments, rather than pretraining on the full 1.28M-image training set.
  • Same total epoch budget — every strategy trained for 20 epochs total. Iterative pruning splits this budget across two prune → retrain cycles of 10 epochs each, rather than getting extra training time as an unfair advantage.
  • Same hyperparameters — 1/10th the original learning rate and batch size 16 across all runs.
  • No data augmentation — removes an additional source of run-to-run randomness so results are directly comparable across strategies and ratios.

Results: Criterion & Type

Scaling-based vs. magnitude-based pruning

With retraining held constant, global scaling-based pruning clearly outperformed global magnitude-based pruning at every ratio — magnitude-based pruning collapsed towards 0% Top-1 at higher ratios while scaling-based pruning degraded far more gracefully. This matches the theory: the batch-norm scale γ reflects a channel's actual contribution to the output directly, while weight magnitude is only a proxy.

Global vs. uniform pruning

Using scaling-based pruning as the criterion, global pruning outperformed uniform (layer-wise) pruning, with the gap widening at higher pruning ratios — despite ending up with a similar or even lower total parameter count. Inspecting the per-layer parameter counts showed why: global pruning automatically preserves more channels in the shallow layers, indicating that early layers are more important to MobileNetV2's representational capacity than uniform pruning assumes.

Results: Iterative Pruning

Rather than pruning straight to a high target ratio, iterative pruning reaches it in two smaller steps (e.g. 0.5 → 0.5 again ≈ 0.75 total, 0.7 → 0.7 again ≈ 0.91 total), retraining for 10 epochs after each step within the same 20-epoch overall budget as single-shot retraining. This consistently outperformed one-shot retraining at large total pruning ratios, where removing that many channels in a single step leaves too little capacity for the network to recover from in one pass.

Results: Knowledge Distillation

Knowledge distillation uses the original, unpruned MobileNetV2 as a teacher to guide the pruned student during retraining. Two variants were compared, both using the same combined loss weighting (0.75 × classification + 0.25 × distillation):

VariantDistillation loss
Matching output logits KL-divergence between teacher and student output distributions, softened with a temperature > 1.
Matching intermediate features MSE between teacher and student feature maps at three manually chosen layers (4, 14, 18).

Matching output logits performed slightly better across all pruning ratios and is simpler to implement — matching intermediate features requires manually choosing which layers to align, an extra hyperparameter that didn't pay off here.

Comparing Fine-Tuning Strategies

Putting retraining, iterative pruning, and knowledge distillation side by side: knowledge distillation gave the strongest boost across most pruning ratios, while iterative pruning's advantage only showed up at large pruning ratios, where all strategies otherwise converge to similarly low accuracy.

Improvement Potential

Overfitting from limited fine-tuning data

A quick hyperparameter check (adding horizontal-flip augmentation, increasing batch size) recovered noticeably more accuracy at the same pruning ratio, pointing to overfitting as a limiting factor: the fine-tuning split used here draws from ImageNet1k's 50,000-image validation set, versus the 1.28M images in the full training set. Further hyperparameter tuning and more training data are likely the fastest path to closing the remaining accuracy gap.

Inference speed

Measured on an NVIDIA RTX 3050 Laptop GPU and an Intel i7-11800H CPU, latency improved with pruning ratio on CPU fairly consistently, but only inconsistently on GPU — the irregular channel counts left after pruning don't align well with the GPU's underlying tiling/warp architecture, so the parameter reduction doesn't translate cleanly into GPU speedup without further kernel-level optimization.

Conclusion

  • MobileNetV2 is highly sensitive to pruning — channel-level pruning is an aggressive method for an already-compact model.
  • Scaling-based pruning (batch-norm γ) outperforms magnitude-based pruning as a channel-importance criterion.
  • Global pruning outperforms uniform pruning, revealing that shallow layers matter more than a fixed per-layer ratio assumes.
  • Iterative pruning improves accuracy over one-shot retraining, specifically at large pruning ratios.
  • Knowledge distillation generally improves accuracy of pruned models, and matching output logits is simpler and slightly stronger than matching intermediate features.

Outlook: explore reinforcement-learning-based pruning heuristics in place of fixed magnitude/scaling criteria, distill from a larger teacher model (e.g. ResNet), and extend distillation to intermediate weights rather than just activations.
